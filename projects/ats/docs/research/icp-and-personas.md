# ICP and Personas: Hospitality ATS

---

## Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

### Primary ICP

**Type:** Independent hospitality group operator  
**Scale:** 5–50 locations  
**Staff:** 20–200 per location  
**Geography:** Operating across 2+ countries, emphasis EU/UK/US  
**Sector:** Restaurant groups, hotel collections, pub/bar chains, mixed-format hospitality estates

**What they look like:**
- Not a single site, not a 500-location enterprise chain
- Run by an owner-operator, a family office, or a mid-market PE-backed management team
- Has 1–3 HR generalists at group level; GMs handle local recruiting day-to-day
- Processes 200–2,000 new hires per year across the estate
- Has seasonal variance: summer and/or festive peaks create 30–100% volume spikes
- Operating in multiple jurisdictions (e.g. UK + France + Spain; or US + UK)
- Current stack: LinkedIn, Indeed, perhaps one job board subscription, spreadsheets, email, WhatsApp

**What they are not:**
- Single-site independents (StaffedUp territory)
- 500+ location enterprise chains with dedicated TA teams (Harri/Fourth territory)
- Pure hotel chains with no F&B (narrower use case)
- Gig economy or logistics (different workforce model — Fountain's territory)

**Why this segment is the right ICP:**
The mid-market multi-location group has hit the ceiling of informal tools but is priced out of or over-served by enterprise solutions. Harri starts at ~$875/month and requires implementation. Hireology is custom-priced and US-centric. Fourth requires enterprise commitment. These operators are currently patching together tools that do not talk to each other, losing candidates in the gaps, and repeating seasonal sourcing from scratch every year.

**Trigger events for purchase:**
1. Pre-peak season (Q1 for summer; Q3 for festive): "We need to hire 80 people across 12 sites in the next 6 weeks"
2. Lost a hire to a competitor because response took 3 days
3. Compliance audit or GDPR query surfaced that the current process cannot demonstrate consent or data handling
4. New location opening — the existing informal process doesn't scale to a new geography or jurisdiction
5. New group HR hire who has used a proper ATS elsewhere and won't go back

**Buyer vs user split:**
- **Buyer:** Group HR Director, People Director, COO, or owner-operator (signs the contract)
- **Daily user:** Unit GMs, assistant managers, HR admin (uses the product every day — must love it or adoption fails)
- **Stakeholder:** CEO / MD (cares about unfilled positions as revenue leakage; sees cost-per-hire on a monthly basis)

## Secondary ICP

**Type:** Seasonal destination property operator  
**Scale:** 1–10 properties  
**Staff:** 20–300 per property (large seasonal swing — e.g., 40 off-season, 200 peak)  
**Geography:** EU coastal/ski; UK; US resort markets  
**Sector:** Hotels, resorts, holiday parks, ski chalets, coastal restaurants

**What distinguishes them from primary ICP:**
- The seasonal swing is more extreme: properties may literally close and reopen
- Talent pool re-engagement is not a feature — it is the core job to be done
- They hire the same population of people year after year (local community, students, returning seasonal workers) and need a system that knows this
- Compliance complexity around fixed-term and seasonal contracts is material

## Persona 1: Siobhán, Group People Director

**Role:** Head of People, mid-size restaurant/hotel group  
**Company:** 18 locations across UK and Ireland  
**Staff:** ~1,400 total  
**Age range:** 35–48  
**Reports to:** COO / CEO  
**Owns:** HR strategy, compliance, culture, hiring process  

**Context**  
Siobhán has been in the role for 2 years. She came from a larger hotel group where they used Hireology. She joined this company and found hiring managed via individual manager WhatsApp groups, a shared email inbox for CV submissions, and a spreadsheet no one maintained. She tried to get Harri approved; the budget committee said no. She's been making do with a basic Indeed package and manual tracking.

**The job she needs done:**  
*"When I wake up on a Monday, I need to know which of my 18 locations has a staffing crisis — not after someone calls me about it, before it becomes a problem."*

**Functional jobs:**
- Monitor hiring pipeline health across all 18 sites from one screen
- Ensure every application gets responded to within 2 hours (candidates go elsewhere fast)
- Run a compliant hiring process: GDPR consent captured, right-to-work documents collected, no discriminatory screening
- Re-hire last summer's good performers without starting from scratch
- Produce a board report on time-to-fill and cost-per-hire at the end of each quarter

**Emotional jobs:**
- Stop feeling like she doesn't know what's happening at half her locations
- Have something to show the CEO that quantifies the hiring function's contribution
- Not be the one who gets called at 9pm because a location is three people short for a Saturday service

**Frustration triggers (sourced from Harri/Hireology/G2 review patterns):**
- "I can't see which location has a bottleneck until someone rings me"
- "Our managers are supposed to use [tool] but half of them still just text me CVs"
- "The GDPR thing keeps me up at night — if someone submits an SAR about a rejected application, I genuinely don't know if we can demonstrate compliance"
- "We don't have a budget for a six-figure platform. I need something that actually works and doesn't cost more than the problem it solves"

**Job story:**  
When I'm heading into peak hiring season for summer, I want to activate job postings across all 18 sites simultaneously with pre-approved role templates and automated screening questions, so that every application is captured, responded to immediately, and visible to the right manager — without me or my team manually routing a single application.

**What she'll pay:**  
£300–600/month for the group (location-based pricing makes sense to her — she can justify per-location cost in her budget). Not opposed to annual contract if the trial de-risks it. Won't buy without a 2-week trial or demo with real data.

**Decision process:**  
Researches independently, demos 2–3 options, gets a reference from a peer operator, presents business case to COO. Approves within 30 days if there's budget.

## Persona 2: Marco, Restaurant Group General Manager

**Role:** GM, 3-location Italian restaurant group in the Netherlands  
**Company:** 3 locations (Amsterdam + Rotterdam + Utrecht), family-owned  
**Staff:** ~90 total  
**Age range:** 30–42  
**Reports to:** Owner  
**Owns:** Day-to-day operations; hiring at all 3 sites is also his responsibility

**Context**  
Marco has no dedicated HR. The owner doesn't want to hire one until the group hits 5 sites. He is the manager, the scheduler, the recruiter, and the compliance officer. He posts on Indeed, responds to applications by email when he can, and interviews people between the lunch and dinner service. He's lost three good candidates in the last quarter because he didn't see their application quickly enough.

**The job he needs done:**  
*"I need to hire a kitchen porter and two servers in the next two weeks. I don't have time to do 47 steps. I just need to know who applied, talk to the good ones fast, and get them started."*

**Functional jobs:**
- Post a job in under 5 minutes from his phone
- See applications the moment they come in, not when he gets back to the office
- Send an interview invitation in 2 taps
- Collect a signed contract and right-to-work check before the first shift
- Not have to log into a desktop system — he doesn't sit at a desk

**Emotional jobs:**
- Feel like he's in control of his team, not constantly scrambling
- Stop apologising to the owner for short-staffed services
- Not feel like the ATS is something designed for a 200-person HR department

**Frustration triggers:**
- "I had three strong candidates apply last week. By the time I saw them, two had already taken jobs elsewhere"
- "The system we tried before wanted me to log in, create an account, complete a 30-page onboarding — I'm a GM, not a HR manager"
- "My owner wants to open a fourth location. I have no idea how I'd hire for it without help"
- "The Dutch labour market is tight right now. I can't afford to be slow"

**Job story:**  
When I get a new application on my phone during a service, I want to be able to flag it as a priority and send an automated "We'll be in touch within 2 hours" message in one tap, so the candidate doesn't go dark before I can call them between services.

**What he'll pay:**  
€79–149/month for 3 locations feels manageable. He will not sign an annual contract without a free trial. He will not pay per-seat or per-hire — he needs predictability. He'll pay for something that saves him 3 hours a week.

**Decision process:**  
Solo decision. Googles "best hiring app restaurant Netherlands" or similar. Tries it. If it works, he keeps it. If not, cancels immediately. Chooses on first-time experience — not feature lists.

## Persona 3: Claire, Head of HR, Seasonal Hotel Group

**Role:** HR Manager (sole HR professional), UK coastal hotel group  
**Company:** 6 properties across South West England; closed November–March  
**Staff:** ~450 in peak season; ~80 off-season  
**Age range:** 38–50  
**Reports to:** Managing Director  
**Owns:** Full HR function solo — hiring, compliance, onboarding, ER

**Context**  
Claire manages the entire HR function for a group that goes from 80 staff in February to 450 staff by mid-June. Every year she starts the same sourcing process from scratch. She uses Indeed and Hospitality Online. She has a folder of CVs from last year but no system to resurface them. She spends March and April in a state of controlled panic. She has used spreadsheets for 7 years and knows it's not working, but she has had no time to evaluate alternatives.

**The job she needs done:**  
*"I need to re-hire 70% of last summer's good workers before I even touch new sourcing — and I need to know which ones to prioritise before I send a single message."*

**Functional jobs:**
- Maintain a live talent pool of past seasonal workers with re-hire flags and performance notes
- Send batch re-engagement messages to all 200+ past workers in early February with one action
- Track who has confirmed availability for which property, which dates, and which role
- Issue fixed-term seasonal contracts digitally — no printing, no scanning
- Demonstrate right-to-work compliance for every new starter before their first shift
- Brief the MD on how many roles are still open by property as of a given date

**Emotional jobs:**
- Not feel like she's rebuilding the entire workforce from zero every spring
- Have evidence to support the budget request for a better system
- Not be the sole person responsible when something goes wrong in a compliance audit

**Frustration triggers:**
- "I spent three weeks last March ringing through a spreadsheet of 200 people I hired two years ago to find out who was available. Two of them had moved. Forty didn't answer. Twenty said yes. That's my talent pool."
- "We have great seasonal staff who come back every year — but I have no way to fast-track them. They go through the same application process as someone I've never met."
- "If a guest had a bad experience because we were underst affed in July, it's partly because I couldn't get confirmation from casual staff quickly enough in April"
- "GDPR. I'm pretty sure we're holding CVs from four years ago and I haven't gotten proper consent for most of them."

**Job story:**  
When I close the properties in November, I want to tag every departing employee as "re-hire eligible", "conditional re-hire", or "do not re-engage", and have the system automatically queue re-engagement messages to the eligible pool in early February with an availability form — so I arrive at March with a partially filled roster instead of an empty one.

**What she'll pay:**  
£150–350/month for the group. Flexible on annual commitment if the seasonal re-engagement feature works as described — that alone justifies the cost vs her current time investment. Will need a demo with a real seasonal use-case walk-through.

**Decision process:**  
MD gives her discretionary budget for tools under £500/month. She evaluates independently. Key criterion: does it solve the seasonal re-hire problem specifically? Generic "great ATS" features are secondary. Will need to see the talent pool / CRM feature in a demo before committing.

## Persona 4: Yusuf, Operations Director (Owner-Operator, No Dedicated HR)

**Role:** Operations Director / Co-owner, casual dining group  
**Company:** 8 locations across the Midlands and North England  
**Staff:** ~320 total  
**Age range:** 42–55  
**Reports to:** No one (co-owner)  
**Owns:** All of operations; hiring decisions escalate to him on anything above supervisor level

**Context**  
Yusuf and his business partner opened their first site in 2009. They now run 8. They have never had dedicated HR. Site managers handle their own hiring with Indeed and social media. Yusuf reviews CVs for supervisor and assistant manager roles only. He has no idea how much hiring is costing him across the group or how long positions typically go unfilled. He just knows that two of his sites have been short-staffed for weeks and it's affecting reviews.

**The job he needs done:**  
*"I need to know — right now — which of my 8 sites has a hiring problem, and I need my managers to be able to act on it without calling me."*

**Functional jobs:**
- A one-screen view of all 8 sites' hiring pipeline: open roles, applications received, interviews scheduled, offers outstanding
- Enable site managers to hire independently within his standards — without requiring him to be in the loop for every application
- See cost-per-hire and time-to-fill by site at month end
- Know which sites have chronic churn vs occasional openings

**Emotional jobs:**
- Feel like he has his finger on the pulse without being a bottleneck
- Stop having to discover staffing crises reactively (usually when a site manager rings him on a Saturday)
- Have something to point at when a new investor or bank asks "what's your HR process?"

**Frustration triggers:**
- "My managers are good operators. They're not good HR people. I need a tool that makes it easy for them — not one that requires training."
- "I don't know what it costs us to hire. I genuinely don't. That's embarrassing."
- "We've been saying we'll fix the hiring process for two years. The reality is no one has time to set something up."
- "I looked at [Harri] once. The demo was great but they wanted more per month than we make on a slow Tuesday."

**Job story:**  
When I log in on a Sunday evening, I want to see a status dashboard that shows me how many open roles each site has, how many applications are in progress, and which ones are stalled — so I can send a quick message to one or two managers rather than making eight phone calls on a Monday morning.

**What he'll pay:**  
£200–500/month for the group. Willing to pay for something that quantifies ROI. Does not want to negotiate with a sales team — will not pick up the phone for a demo if he can self-serve. Decides quickly once he sees the dashboard working.

**Decision process:**  
Self-serve trial or free trial signup. Needs to see the multi-location dashboard work in the first 15 minutes or he will not continue. Referred by a peer operator or finds via organic search. Buys without a long sales cycle if the product demonstrates value immediately.

## Persona 5: The Candidate — Aisha, Looking for a Serving Job

**Role:** Candidate, 22 years old, student and part-time hospitality worker  
**Location:** London  
**Device:** iPhone  
**Communication preference:** WhatsApp + Instagram DM; email rarely checked

**Context**  
Aisha works two days a week at a café. She's looking for a second job — evenings and weekends. She has applied to four places this week using her phone. She gave up on two of them because the application form asked her to upload a CV, create an account, and fill in work history going back 5 years. One sent her an automated email at 9am — she barely uses email. One sent her a WhatsApp message within an hour — she booked an interview the same day. She's interviewing there tomorrow.

**The job she needs done:**  
*"Let me tell you quickly what I can do and when I'm available — and then tell me if you're interested."*

**Functional jobs:**
- Apply in under 3 minutes from her phone
- No account creation, no uploading documents before an interview
- Get a response via WhatsApp (not email)
- Know quickly if she's in or out — not wait 2 weeks for a decision

**Emotional jobs:**
- Feel like the employer actually wants her, not just needs a warm body
- Not feel like applying for a waiting job is more work than applying for a grad scheme
- Know what the job pays and what the hours are before she invests time in an interview

**Frustration triggers (from r/KitchenConfidential, Poached Jobs blog, and Checkr industry data):**
- "Why do I need to create an account just to apply for a weekend shift?"
- "They asked me to upload a CV for a server job. I don't have a CV. I'm 22."
- "I applied to six places and only one got back to me. Now I've taken that job. The other five can keep their email invites."
- "They sent me an email three days later asking if I'm still interested. I started the job already."

**Job story:**  
When I see a job posting I like on Instagram, I want to be able to apply in under 3 minutes without creating an account, and get a WhatsApp message back within the hour telling me what the next step is — so I can make a decision before I get distracted or another employer reaches out first.

**Why this persona matters for product design:**  
Aisha's behaviour is why mobile-first, no-login applications and WhatsApp communication aren't optional features — they are the price of entry for candidate engagement in the EU/UK market. Every additional step in the application funnel loses 20–30% of applicants like Aisha. The ATS that makes her experience fast and frictionless wins the employer the hire.

## ICP and Persona Summary

| Persona | Title | Company Size | Primary Pain | JTBD Step Most Critical | Willingness to Pay |
|---------|-------|-------------|-------------|------------------------|-------------------|
| Siobhán | People Director | 18 locations | Visibility + compliance | S7 Pipeline + S5 Hire | £300–600/month group |
| Marco | Restaurant GM | 3 locations | Speed + mobile | S3 Communicate + S4 Schedule | €79–149/month group |
| Claire | HR Manager (seasonal) | 6 seasonal properties | Talent pool re-engagement | S1 Source (warm pool) + S6 Onboard | £150–350/month group |
| Yusuf | Owner-Operator | 8 locations | Overview + delegation | S7 Pipeline (dashboard) | £200–500/month group |
| Aisha | Candidate | n/a — applies from phone | Speed + simplicity of application | S3 candidate-side | n/a — not the buyer |

**Critical design principle emerging from personas:**  
There are two different user types with deeply different UX requirements: **Siobhán and Yusuf** need a professional desktop dashboard with data and compliance features. **Marco and site managers** need a mobile app that takes 30 seconds to action a candidate. The ATS must serve both without being bloated. This is a product design risk — over-indexing on the dashboard user produces a tool managers hate; over-indexing on simplicity produces a tool the People Director can't justify to the board.
